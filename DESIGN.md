## Problem Statement

Restaurants pay $500+/mo across two vendors: a website provider (BentoBox, Squarespace) and a reservation system (Resy, OpenTable). These are two products that should be one. The website is a template. The booking is a widget. Neither handles special events well. Restaurants are overpaying for commodity software.

## Product Shape: Three Entry Points

This is a hospitality platform with three user types and three entry points:

1. **Restaurant owner** → site + reservations. The core SaaS. Cheap, beautiful, booking built in. This is what you sell.
2. **Restaurant doing events** → event marketing layer on top of their site. Optional. Promotes wine dinners, chef's tables, tasting menus, pop-ups. Discovery for diners.
3. **Supper club organizer** → the poll/coordination tool (already built). Private dining club management: date polling, member coordination, seating. May or may not involve a restaurant on the platform.

The connection: businesses are venues. Event organizers and supper club hosts are customers of those venues. The event/poll system can drive bookings into the business's reservation system, but doesn't have to.

**Beyond restaurants:** The site + booking platform generalizes to any appointment-based local business: spas, tattoo shops, dentists, chiropractors. Restaurant is the first vertical. The booking primitives (time slots, capacity, services, client history) apply across all of them. Build for restaurants first, extract the general platform.

## Demand Evidence

A restaurant owner/manager directly expressed need for this. The current stack of Resy + BentoBox costs $500+/mo and still requires spreadsheets for special events, private dining, and anything that doesn't fit a standard "party of 4 at 7pm" reservation.

## Status Quo

Restaurants cobble together: Resy or OpenTable for reservations ($249-899/mo, plus per-cover fees), BentoBox or Squarespace for their website ($50-200/mo), spreadsheets or email for special events and private dining, Instagram DMs for overflow. The expensive parts (booking platforms) actively compete with the restaurant's brand by steering diners to other restaurants on the same platform.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

Independent restaurants (not chains) paying for both a website provider and a reservation system. The wedge: one product that replaces both, for less. Events and special dining are the differentiator, not the core. The core is: beautiful branded website + reservations, one bill, fraction of the cost.

## Constraints

- Must look as good as BentoBox sites: hero imagery, menu sections with course groupings, event cards with booking CTAs. Mobile-first responsive design (most diners book on phones).
- Must handle real reservation flow: party size, time slots, table assignment, conflict prevention (no double-booking), confirmations, cancellations, no-show tracking.
- Custom domains eventually, but v1 ships on `platform.com/r/[slug]` (already built). Custom domains via Vercel Domains API is a post-launch enhancement.
- Must be cheaper than Resy + BentoBox combined ($500+/mo is the bar to beat).
- Special events (tasting menus, wine dinners, pop-ups, supper clubs) as first-class content. Events have a lifecycle: create, publish, book, sold-out/waitlist, post-event.

## Premises

1. Restaurants are paying $500+/mo across two vendors for something that should be one product.
2. Price is the wedge. Same capabilities, fraction of the cost.
3. Events/special dining is the differentiator that Resy/OpenTable handle poorly.
4. Multi-tenant SaaS: each restaurant gets a branded site with booking built in.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Multi-tenant SaaS (chosen)
Full platform where each restaurant gets a branded site with built-in reservations and events. Template-driven, custom domain support. Go straight to the product.
- Effort: M (schema and booking logic already built)
- Pros: Replaces two vendors. Restaurants own their brand. Events built in.
- Cons: Competing on two fronts (website + booking) simultaneously.

### Approach B: Embeddable Booking Widget
Skip the website, just be a cheaper booking tool with event pages.
- Effort: S
- Pros: No switching cost. Faster to ship.
- Cons: Doesn't replace BentoBox. Smaller value prop. Race to zero on price.

### Approach C: One Restaurant First
Build one perfect site for one restaurant, then extract the template.
- Effort: S-M
- Pros: Real feedback from real usage.
- Cons: Could get stuck in agency mode.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach A: Multi-tenant SaaS.** The schema and routes already exist. The `/r/[slug]/` pattern is in place. Table management, booking forms, and restaurant actions are built. The gap is: polished restaurant-facing site templates, onboarding flow, and pricing.

## What Exists Today

- Multi-tenant schema in `schema.sql` (clients, businesses, bookings, services, tables, menus)
- Restaurant routes at `/r/[slug]/` with admin and booking pages
- `restaurant-actions.ts` with booking, client management, table assignment
- `restaurant-queries.ts` for business and booking data
- `booking-form.tsx` for the reservation flow
- **Poll/supper club system** — fully working. Date polling, member voting with preference ranking, Borda scoring, slot confirmation, seating assignment, email invites. This is entry point #3, already shipped.

The supper club tool and the restaurant platform share a database and user model (clients table). A supper club member who also dines at a restaurant on the platform is the same person.

## What Needs to Be Built

1. **Restaurant site template** — public-facing branded pages (home, menu, about, events, reservations). Must look as good as BentoBox.
2. **Event listings** — special events as first-class content (not just reservations). Title, description, date, capacity, price, booking.
3. **Restaurant onboarding** — sign up, configure your site (name, hours, logo, menu), go live.
4. **Custom domain support** — each restaurant on their own domain.
5. **Dashboard** — restaurant owner view: upcoming reservations, event bookings, guest history (CRM already partially built).
6. **Auth & access** — Restaurant owner login (magic links, already in stack for organizers). Staff roles deferred to post-v1.
7. **Email confirmations** — booking confirmations, event reminders (Resend already in stack).
8. **Pricing & billing** — deferred. Invoice customer #1 manually or Stripe payment link. Full billing after validation.

## Open Questions

- What's the price point? $99/mo? $149/mo? Needs to be obviously cheaper than $500+/mo.
- How much template customization do restaurants need? (colors/logo/photos, or full layout control?)
- What's the onboarding flow? Self-serve, or do you set up the first few manually?
- Data import: can restaurants bring guest lists from Resy/OpenTable via CSV, or is it a fresh start?

## Out of Scope (v1)

- Discovery/marketplace (each restaurant drives its own traffic)
- Custom domains (ship on platform URLs first)
- Full Stripe billing (manual invoicing for early customers)
- Staff roles / multi-user access per restaurant

## Success Criteria

- One restaurant live on the platform with a branded site and working reservations
- Restaurant owner can manage bookings, events, and menus from the dashboard
- Total cost to the restaurant is under $200/mo
- Site looks competitive with BentoBox templates

## Distribution Plan

- Vercel deployment (already in place)
- Custom domain support via Vercel Domains API
- Direct sales to independent restaurants (the person who needs it is customer #1)

## Dependencies

- Email transactional flow (Resend already in stack)
- One real restaurant willing to be customer #1

## The Assignment

Get one real restaurant live on the platform. Not a demo, not a seed database... a real restaurant with real hours, real menus, and real guests booking through it. That's the only evidence that matters.
